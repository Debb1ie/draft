Understanding Teeth

The Anatomy of a Tooth

Every tooth is a complex structure made of several distinct layers, each playing a crucial role in function and long-term protection.

Enamel

The hardest substance in the human body, enamel forms the outer protective shell of the crown. It is 96% mineral and shields the inner layers from decay, acids, and physical wear over a lifetime.

Dentin

Beneath the enamel lies dentin, a yellowish porous tissue that makes up the majority of the tooth. It contains microscopic tubules that transmit sensations to the nerve, causing sensitivity when exposed.

Pulp

The living core of the tooth. The pulp chamber houses blood vessels, nerves, and connective tissue. It nourishes the tooth and signals pain when infection or structural damage occurs.

Cementum

A bone-like tissue covering the root surface, cementum anchors the periodontal ligament connecting the tooth to the jawbone, providing stability and slight flexibility during chewing.

Periodontal Ligament

A network of fibrous connective tissue that suspends the tooth within its bony socket. It cushions bite forces and contains sensory receptors that help gauge chewing pressure.

Alveolar Bone

The jawbone forming the sockets that house the tooth roots. It continuously remodels in response to bite forces and is the structure that anchors dental implants in restorative procedures.

Dental Education

What Dentists Study for 8+ Years

Becoming a dentist requires deep mastery of biology, anatomy, medicine, and hands-on clinical skill.

Years 1–2 · Pre-clinical Sciences

Anatomy & Histology

Intensive study of head, neck, and oral structures — bones, muscles, nerves, and blood vessels — plus microscopic examination of all dental tissues.

Years 1–2 · Biomedical Foundations

Microbiology & Pathology

How bacteria, fungi, and viruses cause oral disease. Understanding immune responses, inflammation, infection, and tissue disease at a cellular level.

Years 2–3 · Applied Sciences

Pharmacology & Radiology

Drug interactions, local anesthetics, antibiotics for dental use. Reading X-rays, CBCT scans, and understanding radiation safety in diagnosis.

Years 3–4 · Clinical Practice

Operative Dentistry & Surgery

Hands-on cavity preparation, restorations, extractions, and minor oral surgery under supervised clinical rotations with real patients.

Your 32 Teeth

Types of Teeth & Their Roles

Each type of tooth has a unique shape perfectly designed for its specific function in biting, tearing, and grinding food.

8 teeth

Incisors

The 8 front teeth with a sharp, flat edge for biting and cutting food. First to emerge in infants around 6 months of age.

4 teeth

Canines

Pointed corner teeth designed to grip, tear, and puncture food. They have the longest roots of any tooth in your mouth.

8 teeth

Premolars

Bicuspids with two cusps and a flat surface for crushing and tearing food. They transition between canines and molars.

8 teeth

Molars

Large back teeth with multiple cusps for grinding food into small, digestible pieces. They bear the heaviest chewing forces.

4 teeth

Wisdom Teeth

Third molars emerging in late teens or 20s. Often impacted or misaligned, frequently requiring extraction when they cause problems.

Daily Oral Care

Essential Dental Care Tips

Healthy teeth for life come down to consistent daily habits. These evidence-based practices make the biggest difference.

01

Brush Twice Daily — Correctly

Use a soft-bristled brush at a 45° angle to the gumline. Brush for at least 2 minutes using gentle circular motions. Fluoride toothpaste is essential for enamel protection.

02

Floss Every Single Day

Brushing only cleans 60% of tooth surfaces. Flossing removes plaque and food from between teeth and under the gumline where cavities love to form.

03

Limit Sugar & Acidic Foods

Bacteria feed on sugars and produce acid that erodes enamel. Soda, candy, and sports drinks are the biggest culprits. Rinse with water after consuming them.

04

Stay Hydrated — Drink Water

Saliva neutralizes acids, washes away bacteria, and remineralizes enamel. Fluoridated tap water also directly strengthens tooth enamel over time.

05

Visit Your Dentist Regularly

Professional cleanings remove tartar that brushing cannot reach. Early detection of cavities, gum disease, and oral cancer dramatically improves outcomes.

06

Wear a Mouthguard When Needed

If you grind your teeth at night or play contact sports, a custom mouthguard protects against fractures, wear, and jaw joint damage. Ask your dentist for a fitted guard.
